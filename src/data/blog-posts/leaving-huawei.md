## Leaving Huawei — comfort, structure, and deep technical exposure

Working at Huawei was my first real step into enterprise technology environments.

It was structured, fast-paced, and deeply technical. I worked on automation, business processes, and supporting telecom operations across different systems and teams. There was always something to fix, improve, or optimize.

Over time, I became familiar with:

* workflow automation and reporting systems
* telecom site and operations support
* working with structured enterprise processes
* troubleshooting real operational issues under pressure

It was a place where I learned discipline through execution. You didn’t just design solutions — you implemented them in systems that affected real users and operations.

Eventually, though, I started feeling a shift. I didn’t just want to build and support systems — I wanted to understand broader business problems, how decisions are made, and how technology fits into strategy.

That curiosity became the bridge to my next step.

## The decision to move — stepping into uncertainty

Leaving a familiar environment is never just a career decision. It is also an identity shift.

Huawei had structure. I knew the systems, the expectations, and the workflows. I knew how to operate effectively within that space.

But KPMG represented something different — less about fixed systems, and more about ambiguity, clients, and advisory thinking.

The decision wasn’t instant or easy. It came with questions like:

* Am I ready for a less technical but more strategic environment?
* Can I adapt from execution-focused work to problem framing and consulting?
* What does growth look like in a space where answers are not predefined?

In hindsight, those questions were part of the reason I needed the move.

## Entering KPMG — learning to think differently

The transition into consulting required a mental shift.

At Huawei, success often meant:

* building solutions
* resolving technical issues
* improving systems and processes

In consulting, success started to look different:

* understanding client problems clearly
* structuring ambiguous information
* communicating insights effectively
* connecting technology to business outcomes

One of the biggest adjustments was learning to slow down and think in structure before jumping into solutions.

Consulting taught me that how you frame a problem often determines the quality of the answer.

## From execution to advisory thinking

One of the most important lessons in this transition was moving from execution-first thinking to structured problem solving.

In technical environments, it is natural to want to “fix” things quickly.

In consulting, I learned to first ask:

* What exactly is the problem?
* Who is impacted?
* What does success look like?
* What constraints exist?

This shift changed how I approach everything, not just work, but decision-making in general.

## What I carried from Huawei into KPMG

Even though the environments are different, Huawei gave me a strong foundation that still shapes how I work today:

* Discipline in handling structured systems
* Experience with real operational challenges
* Comfort with technical depth
* Exposure to automation and process improvement
* Problem-solving under pressure

These became transferable strengths in consulting environments, especially when dealing with technology-related discussions.

## What KPMG is teaching me

If Huawei taught me execution, KPMG is teaching me perspective.

Some of the key lessons so far:

* clarity is more valuable than complexity
* communication is a technical skill in its own right
* business context matters as much as technical accuracy
* structured thinking reduces ambiguity
* impact is measured differently across stakeholders

It has been less about replacing what I knew, and more about expanding how I think.

## Final thoughts

Transitioning from Huawei to KPMG wasn’t just a job change, it was a shift in how I approach technology and problem solving.

I moved from building and supporting systems to understanding and shaping decisions around them.

And in that shift, I’ve learned something important:

Growth is not always about moving away from what you know, sometimes it is about building on it in a new direction.
