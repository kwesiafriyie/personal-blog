## Lately, I’ve really wanted to learn how to skate

Not casually interested.

Actually interested.

The kind where you keep watching skate videos, imagining yourself cruising smoothly somewhere, while fully aware that your current reality is probably one bad balance away from embarrassment.

Still, the interest has stayed with me for a while now.

And I think I’ve decided I’m finally going to do it.

---

## The interesting part is that I already know someone willing to teach me

Which removes the biggest excuse.

I already know someone who’s genuinely good at skating and is willing to teach me properly.

So now the only real obstacle is consistency, courage, and convincing my body to cooperate with physics.

---

## Why skating though?

Honestly, I don’t fully know.

Part of it is curiosity.
Part of it is wanting to learn something completely outside my normal world of systems, consulting, deadlines, and screens.

Skating feels different.

It looks freeing.
Creative.
Unstructured.

There’s also something humbling about being bad at something again.

As adults, especially in professional environments, we slowly avoid beginner situations because we’re used to competence.

Skating completely destroys that comfort.

And maybe that’s part of the appeal.

---

## Saturdays are the only real window

Right now, Saturdays are realistically the only time I can go to the park consistently.

Which honestly fits the mood of skating anyway.

Slow weekends.
Open space.
Music somewhere in the background.
People falling and laughing like it’s part of the process.

I haven’t even properly started yet, but I already like the atmosphere around it.

---

## I already know the first few sessions will be humbling

I fully expect:
- terrible balance
- awkward falls
- overconfidence followed by immediate regret
- and muscles hurting in places I forgot existed

But I think that’s good.

Not everything needs to become productive or optimized immediately.

Some things are worth doing simply because they make life feel wider.

---

## Learning things as an adult feels different

When you’re younger, learning happens constantly without much thought.

As adults, trying something new feels more intentional.

You have to create time for it.
Protect the space for it.
Allow yourself to be inexperienced again.

And I think there’s something healthy about that.

---

## Final thoughts

Maybe months from now I’ll look back at this post while skating comfortably.

Or maybe I’ll still be struggling to stay on the board for more than thirty seconds.

Either way, I’m glad I’m trying.

Because lately I’ve realized that life shouldn’t only be work and responsibilities.

Sometimes it should also include learning random things that make you feel alive again.
